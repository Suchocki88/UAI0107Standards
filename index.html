Lesson 1.7 Standards Alignment

Intelligence All Around Us
Type: Discussion Day
Chapter: 1
Duration: 50 minutes

Lesson 1.7 is a discussion-based capstone for Chapter 1, where students synthesize their learning through reflection, discourse, and critical thinking about AI's societal implications. This lesson emphasizes oral communication, collaborative discussion, and the application of inquiry skills to complex real-world issues.

Common Core State Standards - English Language Arts

Lesson 1.7 strongly emphasizes speaking and listening standards as students engage in collaborative discussion, synthesize learning, and communicate complex ideas about AI's societal impact.

Speaking and Listening Standards (Grades 9-10)

CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.9-10.1
Initiate and participate effectively in a range of collaborative discussions (one-on-one, in groups, and teacher-led) with diverse partners on grades 9-10 topics, texts, and issues, building on others' ideas and expressing their own clearly and persuasively.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students engage in pair-share discussions and whole-class dialogue about AI's nature, capabilities, and societal implications. They build on classmates' ideas about environmental, economic, and social impacts of AI technology.
CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.9-10.1.A
Come to discussions prepared, having read and researched material under study; explicitly draw on that preparation by referring to evidence from texts and other research on the topic or issue to stimulate a thoughtful, well-reasoned exchange of ideas.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students reference their field journal documentation from Lessons 1.3-1.5, citing specific observations from their Alia experiments to support claims about AI capabilities and limitations.
CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.9-10.1.C
Propel conversations by posing and responding to questions that relate the current discussion to broader themes or larger ideas; actively incorporate others into the discussion; and clarify, verify, or challenge ideas and conclusions.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Discussion questions connect specific AI observations to broader themes: environmental sustainability, economic equity, social justice. Students challenge and build on each other's ideas about AI's "moving target" nature.
CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.9-10.1.D
Respond thoughtfully to diverse perspectives, summarize points of agreement and disagreement, and, when warranted, qualify or justify their own views and understanding and make new connections in light of the evidence and reasoning presented.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students discuss competing values (e.g., AI's energy consumption vs. its problem-solving potential), acknowledge different perspectives on economic impacts, and refine their thinking based on classmates' insights.
CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.SL.9-10.2
Integrate multiple sources of information presented in diverse media or formats (e.g., visually, quantitatively, orally) evaluating the credibility and accuracy of each source.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students synthesize information from their hands-on experiments (field journals), lesson content, and optional current news articles about AI to form comprehensive understanding of AI's societal role.

Writing Standards (Grades 9-10)

CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.W.9-10.10
Write routinely over extended time frames (time for research, reflection, and revision) and shorter time frames (a single sitting or a day or two) for a range of tasks, purposes, and audiences.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students engage in brief reflective writing (field journal entries) synthesizing Chapter 1 learning and articulating their current understanding of whether AI "thinks."

Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS)

As a discussion day, Lesson 1.7 emphasizes science practices related to argumentation, evidence-based reasoning, and understanding broader impacts of technology.

Science and Engineering Practices

Practice 6: Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions
Construct and revise an explanation based on valid and reliable evidence obtained from a variety of sources.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students construct explanations of AI's nature and capabilities based on evidence gathered through Chapter 1 experiments, revising their initial assumptions based on direct observation.
Practice 7: Engaging in Argument from Evidence
Construct, use, and present an oral and written argument supported by empirical evidence and scientific reasoning to support or refute an explanation or a model for a phenomenon.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students present oral arguments about whether AI "thinks," supported by evidence from their experiments. They engage in reasoned debate about AI's environmental costs vs. benefits, economic impacts, and ethical implications.
Practice 8: Obtaining, Evaluating, and Communicating Information
Critically read scientific literature adapted for classroom use to determine the central ideas or conclusions of a text; summarize complex concepts, processes, or information presented in a text.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students synthesize complex information about AI's rapid evolution, environmental impact (energy consumption data), and societal implications, communicating their understanding through discussion.

Crosscutting Concepts

Stability and Change
Much of science deals with constructing explanations of how things change and how they remain stable.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Discussion of AI as a "moving target" emphasizes rapid technological change while identifying stable core concepts (pattern recognition, training data dependence) that remain constant.
Cause and Effect: Mechanism and Explanation
Events have causes, sometimes simple, sometimes multifaceted.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students explore cause-effect relationships: training data ‚Üí AI capabilities; AI energy use ‚Üí environmental impact; AI automation ‚Üí economic disruption; unequal AI access ‚Üí social inequality.

CSTA K-12 Computer Science Standards

Lesson 1.7 addresses computer science standards related to societal impacts, ethics, and the broader context of computing technologies in human life.

Impacts of Computing

3A-IC-24
Evaluate the ways computing impacts personal, ethical, social, economic, and cultural practices.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Core lesson focus. Students explicitly discuss computing's impacts across domains: environmental (energy consumption), economic (job automation, productivity inequality), social (access inequality, decision-making power), ethical (responsibilities of developers and users).
3A-IC-28
Explain the beneficial and harmful effects that intellectual property laws can have on innovation.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Discussion touches on AI's rapid evolution and questions of who benefits from AI development, connecting to broader questions about technology innovation, access, and intellectual property.
3B-IC-25
Evaluate computational artifacts to maximize their beneficial effects and minimize harmful effects on society.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students evaluate AI as a computational artifact by weighing benefits (problem-solving capabilities, productivity gains) against harms (energy consumption, job displacement, access inequality).
3B-IC-26
Evaluate the impact of equity, access, and influence on the distribution of computing resources in a global society.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Economic impact discussion explicitly addresses unequal access to AI tools and the implications when some people are "supercharged" by AI while others lack access to these productivity-enhancing technologies.
3B-IC-27
Predict how computational innovations that have revolutionized aspects of our culture might evolve.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: "Moving target" discussion addresses AI's rapid evolution and has students predict future capabilities and implications. Students consider how AI that seems cutting-edge today will be primitive compared to future versions.

C3 Framework for Social Studies State Standards

Lesson 1.7 strongly aligns with C3 Framework's emphasis on inquiry, deliberation about public issues, and civic engagement through informed discussion of technology's societal role.

Dimension 1: Developing Questions and Planning Inquiries

D1.2.9-12
Explain points of agreement and disagreement experts have about interpretations and applications of disciplinary concepts and ideas associated with a compelling question.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students explore disagreement about AI's net impact: Some see environmental costs as prohibitive; others emphasize problem-solving benefits. Students examine competing interpretations of AI's economic effects.
D1.5.9-12
Determine the kinds of sources that will be helpful in answering compelling and supporting questions, taking into consideration multiple points of view represented in the sources, the types of sources available, and the potential uses of the sources.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students synthesize multiple sources of evidence: their own experimental observations (field journals), lesson content, optional news articles, and peer perspectives during discussion‚Äîrecognizing value of diverse information sources.

Dimension 2: Applying Disciplinary Concepts - Economics

D2.Eco.1.9-12
Analyze how incentives influence choices that may result in policies with a range of costs and benefits for different groups.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Economic impact discussion examines how incentives drive AI development and deployment, with benefits accruing to those with access while costs (job displacement, energy consumption) are distributed differently across society.
D2.Eco.14.9-12
Analyze how technological innovations have affected economic decision-making and behaviors.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students discuss how AI innovations affect economic decisions: automation of work, productivity enhancements for some workers, changes in competitive advantages for businesses with AI access.

Dimension 3: Evaluating Sources and Using Evidence

D3.1.9-12
Gather relevant information from multiple sources representing a wide range of views while using the origin, authority, structure, context, and corroborative value of the sources to guide the selection.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students draw on field journal evidence (their own authority), lesson content (educational authority), optional news articles (journalistic authority), and peer observations (collaborative authority) to construct understanding.
D3.3.9-12
Identify evidence that draws information directly and substantively from multiple sources to detect inconsistencies in evidence in order to revise or strengthen claims.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students identify evidence from their experiments that either confirms or challenges their initial assumptions about AI, revising their understanding based on direct observation and peer discussion.

Dimension 4: Communicating Conclusions and Taking Informed Action

D4.1.9-12
Construct arguments using precise and knowledgeable claims, with evidence from multiple sources, while acknowledging counterclaims and evidentiary weaknesses.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students construct arguments about whether AI "thinks" using evidence from experiments while acknowledging alternative interpretations. Environmental/economic discussions require weighing competing claims with supporting evidence.
D4.3.9-12
Present adaptations of arguments and explanations that feature evocative ideas and perspectives on issues and topics to reach a range of audiences and venues outside the classroom.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Students practice articulating complex ideas about AI to peers with varying perspectives, developing communication skills applicable beyond the classroom to civic discourse about technology policy.
D4.7.9-12
Assess options for individual and collective action to address local, regional, and global problems by engaging in self-reflection, strategy identification, and complex causal reasoning.
Lesson 1.7 Connection: Discussion prompts students to consider their role as citizens in shaping AI development and regulation: "What voice should citizens have in how AI is developed?" "What responsibilities do you have as someone who understands AI?"
